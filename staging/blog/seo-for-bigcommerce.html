SEO for BigCommerce: The Complete Guide

BigCommerce SEO fundamentals - an expansion page testing non-Shopify demand.

BBuffy Intel2026-07-09 · 7 min read

Overview

SEO for BigCommerce: the complete guide starts with one basic fact - BigCommerce isn't Shopify with a different name on the invoice. It ships with more native SEO controls out of the box: editable robots.txt, native 301 redirects, per-page schema markup in Stencil themes, and full title/meta/URL fields on every product and category without an app. That's a real advantage if you use it, and a wasted one if you don't.

Why this matters now: DTC brands are increasingly running multi-channel storefronts - B2C, B2B, wholesale, or region-specific catalogs - and BigCommerce's multi-storefront and headless options (Catalyst, Makeswift) make that easier than most platforms. But every added storefront or feed is another source of duplicate content and crawl waste if you don't configure canonicals and redirects deliberately. And if you're scaling into new markets, including fast-growing e-commerce regions like India, you're likely on BigCommerce Professional or Enterprise for a reason - you have the catalog scale where technical SEO mistakes compound fast. A bad faceted-navigation setup on a 5,000-SKU catalog can generate tens of thousands of thin, duplicate URLs that dilute your crawl budget and rankings.

This guide covers the platform-specific mechanics, not generic SEO advice, so you can use what BigCommerce actually gives you instead of fighting it or paying for an app that duplicates a native feature.

What To Know

Before you touch anything, understand five things about how BigCommerce actually works.

URL structure is flatter than Shopify's. Products and categories don't get forced folders like /products/ or /collections/ - you control the full path per page, so you can build clean, keyword-relevant URLs (e.g., /organic-cotton-throw-blankets/ instead of /collections/all/products/throw-blanket-123). Set these once at launch; changing them later means redirects.

Faceted navigation is your biggest duplicate-content risk. BigCommerce's filter system (by price, color, size, brand) generates a unique URL for nearly every filter combination unless you configure it. Go to Storefront > Navigation and set secondary filters to noindex, follow, or rely on the built-in canonical settings so filtered URLs don't compete with your main category page.

Stencil themes ship with schema.org structured data for products (price, availability, reviews) already in the markup - you don't need a separate app for basic Product schema, but verify it in Google's Rich Results Test, because theme customizations often break it.

Every product, category, and page has native title tag, meta description, and URL fields - no app required. That's different from Shopify, where meta fields are often template-level by default.

Multi-storefront (available on Enterprise) lets you run separate catalogs - by brand, region, or B2B/B2C - from one backend. Each storefront needs its own canonical or hreflang strategy or you'll create cross-domain duplicate content.

Page speed benefits from BigCommerce's built-in CDN (Akamai) and server-side rendering, but third-party apps and heavy Stencil customizations still erode Core Web Vitals - audit script bloat quarterly, not just at launch.

Step By Step

  1. Crawl your live store with Screaming Frog or Sitebulb and export every indexed URL. Flag duplicate title tags, thin auto-generated category pages, and any faceted-filter URLs (query strings like ?brand= or ?price=) that Google has indexed.
  1. Lock down faceted navigation. In the control panel, go to Storefront > Navigation > Search Filters and set secondary filter combinations to noindex, follow, or add self-referencing canonicals pointing back to the clean category URL.
  1. Fix your URL structure while your catalog is still manageable. Use short, descriptive paths without stop words or duplicated category names. If you must change existing URLs, create 301 redirects in Route 66 (BigCommerce's native redirect tool) before the old URLs drop out of the index.
  1. Fill in unique title tags, meta descriptions, and product descriptions for every category and top-selling product - BigCommerce gives you the fields natively, so there's no excuse for auto-generated defaults.
  1. Validate structured data with Google's Rich Results Test on at least one product from each template variant you use, since custom Stencil edits frequently strip schema markup.
  1. Submit your sitemap.xml (auto-generated at yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml) in Google Search Console and check the Coverage report monthly for spikes in "duplicate" or "crawled, not indexed" pages - that's your faceted-nav problem resurfacing.
  1. Build internal links from content into money pages. BigCommerce's native blog is thin on SEO features, so write category-page copy that links out to related categories and top products instead of relying on the blog alone.
  1. If you run multiple storefronts, set explicit cross-domain canonicals or hreflang tags between them - this doesn't happen automatically.
  1. Track how your catalog shows up beyond classic search results. Tools like Buffy can show you how AI shopping assistants and answer engines surface, or skip, your products - a growing share of discovery traffic that Search Console won't show you.
  1. Re-crawl quarterly. Catalog changes, app installs, and theme edits are the most common ways BigCommerce stores silently regress on SEO.

Common Mistakes

Leaving faceted navigation wide open is the single most common technical mistake. Brands launch with default filter settings and within months have thousands of indexed URLs for a catalog a fraction that size, most of them thin duplicates. Fix it before launch, not after Search Console flags it.

Treating BigCommerce's native redirect tool as optional. Because URL changes are easy to make, teams restructure categories without redirecting old URLs, creating 404s that lose accumulated link equity and rankings overnight.

Relying on the built-in blog for all content and internal linking. BigCommerce's native blog has limited SEO controls (thin taxonomy, basic schema) - pair it with Stencil-based landing pages or a headless build if content is a real growth channel for you.

Assuming theme customizations don't affect schema markup. Developers frequently strip or duplicate Product schema when they customize Stencil templates for design reasons. Re-test structured data after any theme change, not just at launch.

Ignoring out-of-stock and discontinued product handling. Deleting a product outright returns a 404; the better pattern is a 301 to a relevant replacement or category, or a proper 410 if there's truly no replacement. Dead links accumulate crawl errors fast on large catalogs.

Running multiple storefronts without a canonical or hreflang strategy. Multi-storefront is powerful for regional or B2B expansion, but without cross-domain signals, Google sees near-duplicate catalogs competing against each other instead of one brand benefiting from consolidated authority.

Over-installing apps for things BigCommerce already does natively. Meta tags, redirects, and basic schema don't need a third-party app, and each added script is another hit to page speed.

Faq

Is BigCommerce good for SEO compared to Shopify?

Both platforms can rank well. BigCommerce's advantage is more native technical control - redirects, meta fields, schema, editable robots.txt - without needing apps. Shopify has a larger app and theme ecosystem and is often simpler for non-technical teams to manage day to day. The deciding factor is usually your catalog size and technical resourcing, not an inherent ranking advantage of one platform over the other.

How do I stop BigCommerce filter pages from creating duplicate content?

Go to Storefront > Navigation > Search Filters and set secondary or combination filters to noindex, follow, or make sure they carry a canonical tag back to the base category page. Also check that your robots.txt isn't simply blocking these URLs, since blocking still allows crawling in some cases and can produce "indexed, though blocked" errors in Search Console instead of clean deindexing.

Can I fully customize URLs on BigCommerce?

Yes. BigCommerce doesn't force a /products/ or /collections/ folder structure like Shopify does - you set the full path per page in the URL field. Decide on your structure before launch, since changing URLs later requires manually creating 301 redirects through Route 66, BigCommerce's native redirect tool.

Does BigCommerce automatically generate a sitemap?

Yes, at yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml, and it updates automatically as you add or remove products and categories. Submit it in Google Search Console and recheck it after large catalog changes - the sitemap won't flag orphaned or noindexed pages for you, so it's not a substitute for a manual crawl.

How does structured data work on BigCommerce?

Stencil themes include baseline schema.org markup for Product data (price, availability, ratings) by default, so you don't need a separate app for basic coverage. Verify it with Google's Rich Results Test, especially after any custom theme development, since template edits are the most common way this markup gets broken or duplicated.
