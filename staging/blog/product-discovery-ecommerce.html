Product Discovery in Ecommerce: Beyond the Search Bar

How shoppers actually find products - search, browse, recommendations, and chat - and where stores lose them.

BBuffy Intel2026-07-09 · 7 min read

Overview

"Product Discovery in Ecommerce: Beyond the Search Bar" is about mapping every path a shopper actually takes to find a product on your store - and increasingly, on platforms you don't control. For most of ecommerce's history, discovery meant two things: the search bar and the category nav. That's no longer true. Shoppers now find products through onsite search, browse-and-filter journeys, algorithmic recommendations, social feeds, and - fast-growing - conversational AI: ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews, and voice assistants that summarize and recommend products without a shopper ever touching your site's search bar.

This matters right now because each of those channels runs on different mechanics, and most DTC stores are still tuned for only one or two of them. If your site search can't handle typos or synonyms, if your recommendation widgets are static "best sellers" blocks, or if an AI shopping assistant can't parse your product data cleanly, you're invisible in a growing share of discovery moments - even when your product is exactly what the shopper wants. Winning here isn't one tactic; it's auditing every discovery surface and fixing the ones quietly leaking revenue.

For a DTC brand, this is a margin lever, not a UX nicety. Shoppers who find what they want convert faster and buy more. Every failed discovery moment - a zero-result search, a mis-ranked category page, an AI assistant that recommends a competitor instead of you - is a lost sale that never shows up in your funnel reports, because the shopper never entered your funnel at all.

What To Know

Discovery on a modern store runs through five distinct surfaces, and each fails for different reasons:

  1. Search. The query box, but also predictive/autocomplete. It fails on typo intolerance, missing synonyms ("sneakers" vs. "trainers"), and no handling for natural-language or long-tail queries. Baymard Institute's ongoing ecommerce search benchmarking has repeatedly found that most large retail sites handle these basics poorly, which is a big reason search-driven sessions convert worse than they should.
  1. Browse and navigation. Category pages, faceted filters, and sort order. This is "zero-query" discovery - shoppers who don't have a specific product in mind yet. Most traffic actually falls here, not into the search bar.
  1. Recommendations and merchandising. "Customers also bought," related items, cross-sell modules, and personalized ranking. Static, non-personalized versions of these are common and underperform behavior-based ones.
  1. Social and referral discovery. TikTok Shop, Instagram, influencer links, and reviews that drive a shopper to you already primed with intent - your job is not losing them once they land.
  1. Conversational/AI discovery. Shoppers now ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google's AI Overviews to compare or recommend products. These systems don't browse your site UI - they read structured data, product feeds, and text content. If your catalog isn't legible to them (thin descriptions, missing schema markup, inconsistent specs), you're excluded from an answer before the shopper ever sees a link.

The core shift to internalize: discovery is no longer a single funnel step you optimize once. It's five parallel surfaces, each with its own failure modes, and shoppers move between them within a single session.

Step By Step

  1. Instrument discovery analytics. Tag sessions by entry path - search, category/filter, recommendation click, social referral, or AI/LLM referral - so you can see conversion rate by discovery channel, not just by traffic source. Most analytics setups only show you the last one or two of these.
  1. Audit and fix on-site search. Pull your top zero-result queries weekly. Add synonym mapping, typo tolerance, and merchandising rules (pin or boost specific results for common searches) so a bad search doesn't end the session.
  1. Rebuild filters around customer language, not internal taxonomy. Interview support and sales teams for the vocabulary shoppers actually use, then build facets around use case, occasion, skin type, or room - not the SKU attributes your inventory system uses.
  1. Replace static recommendations with behavior-based ones. Swap generic "best sellers" blocks for recently-viewed, bought-together, and related-by-purchase modules, and A/B test each one against the version it replaces before rolling it out fully.
  1. Prepare your catalog for AI discovery. Add schema.org Product, Offer, and Review markup, keep product feeds (Google Merchant Center and equivalents) current, and write descriptions that directly answer the questions a shopper would ask an AI assistant. This is where a tool like Buffy is useful even if you do everything else manually - it tracks whether and how your products actually surface in AI answers and on-site AI search, so you catch visibility gaps before they cost you sales instead of after.
  1. Assign ownership and a review cadence. Put one person on a monthly discovery review covering zero-result searches, filter usage, recommendation click-through, and AI-referral traffic. Many DTC and mid-market teams staff this as a dedicated ecommerce product manager role, and a growing share hire it out of India's deep product management talent pool rather than splitting the work across marketing and engineering.
  1. Ship and measure one surface at a time. Change one discovery surface, hold a control group, and confirm conversion lift before moving to the next - these surfaces interact, and changing two at once makes it impossible to tell what worked.

Common Mistakes

Treating the search bar as the only discovery investment. Most stores put all their optimization budget into search relevance while browse, filters, and recommendations - which drive more sessions for most catalogs - go untouched.

Not tracking zero-result queries. This is the single biggest leak on most sites: a shopper searches for something you sell, gets nothing back because of a synonym or spelling mismatch, and leaves. If you're not reviewing this list weekly, you're losing sales you'll never see in a report.

Filters built around your inventory system instead of your customer's mental model. Facets organized by internal SKU attributes (material code, vendor ID) instead of how a shopper actually thinks about the purchase (occasion, use case, skin type) create friction that looks like low intent but is actually bad taxonomy.

Static, one-size-fits-all recommendation widgets. A "best sellers" rail that's identical for every visitor isn't personalization - it's a merchandising decision dressed up as one. It underperforms behavior-based recommendations and rarely gets tested against alternatives.

Ignoring conversational/AI discovery entirely. Thin product descriptions, missing structured data, and stale feeds mean AI shopping assistants either can't parse your catalog or default to a competitor's cleaner data. This channel is small for some categories today but growing fast, and it's much cheaper to fix your product data now than to retrofit it after you've noticed the traffic gap.

No single owner. Discovery gets split across marketing (search/SEO), engineering (site search infra), and merchandising (recommendations), with nobody tracking it end to end. Without one person accountable for a discovery roadmap and a regular review cadence, fixes happen reactively - usually only after a competitor visibly out-converts you.

Faq

What is product discovery in ecommerce?

It's every path a shopper uses to find a product before buying: typing a query into site search, browsing categories and filters, clicking a recommendation, arriving from social or influencer content, or - increasingly - getting a product recommended by an AI assistant like ChatGPT or Google's AI Overviews. It's broader than "search," which is only one of these paths.

How do most shoppers find products if not through the search bar?

A large share of sessions start with zero specific query intent - shoppers browse categories, use filters, or click a recommended or related item. Others arrive already primed by a social post, review, or influencer link. Site search still matters, especially for high-intent shoppers, but it's one of several parallel discovery surfaces, not the default entry point for most traffic.

How do I fix a bad on-site search experience?

Start by pulling your zero-result query report weekly - it's the fastest way to find exact phrases shoppers use that return nothing. Add synonym mapping ("couch" = "sofa"), typo tolerance, and merchandising rules to boost or pin key results. Then check whether your search understands natural-language phrasing, since more queries now look like questions than keyword strings.

What is AI product discovery and how does it affect DTC brands?

It's shoppers using conversational AI tools - ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews, voice assistants - to research or get recommended products, often without visiting any retailer's site directly. These systems read your structured data, product feeds, and public content, not your site's UI. If your product pages are thin or your feeds are stale, you can be excluded from the answer entirely, even for products you'd win on merit.

Do ecommerce brands need a dedicated product manager for discovery?

Once you're running search relevance, filter taxonomy, recommendation testing, and AI-feed work at the same time, it's usually too fragmented for marketing or engineering to own part-time. Many DTC and mid-market ecommerce companies address this by hiring a dedicated ecommerce or growth product manager - often sourced from India's large, experienced product management talent pool - to run a single discovery roadmap and a recurring review cadence.
