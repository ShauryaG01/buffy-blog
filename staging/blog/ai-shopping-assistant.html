AI Shopping Assistants: The Complete Guide for Brands & Shoppers

What AI shopping assistants are, how they change buying, and how stores deploy one - the pillar bridging on-site AI and AI-assistant visibility.

BBuffy Intel2026-07-09 · 8 min read

Overview

AI shopping assistants are software agents built on large language models that help someone research, compare, and decide on a purchase through conversation instead of clicking through search results, filters, and category pages. Two versions of this now matter to a DTC brand. The first lives outside your store: ChatGPT's shopping and browsing features, Google's AI Overviews and AI Mode, Perplexity, Amazon's Rufus, and Microsoft Copilot are all fielding product questions that used to go to a search box, then rendering an answer that names, or skips, specific brands and products. The second lives on your own storefront: a conversational search or concierge widget that answers pre-purchase questions, recommends products from your actual catalog, and replaces static filters with natural-language search.

This matters right now because the on-ramp to purchase is moving upstream. Adobe Analytics has documented triple- and quadruple-digit year-over-year growth in U.S. retail traffic referred from generative AI sources during recent holiday shopping periods - still a small slice of total traffic, but growing fast and disproportionately influential, since a shopper arriving via an AI recommendation is typically closer to a buying decision than one arriving via a generic search click. If your product data isn't structured so these systems can read it, verify it, and trust it, you don't rank lower - you simply don't get mentioned. That shift is global: brands selling to professional buyers, and brands expanding into fast-growing, mobile-first markets like India, are exposed to it whether or not they've noticed yet. This guide covers both sides: how these assistants actually work, and how to show up in them - as a recommended product off-site, and as a genuinely useful assistant on-site.

What To Know

Split the landscape into two categories, because the mechanics and the levers you control differ for each.

Off-site (model-side) assistants - ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews/AI Mode, Gemini, Amazon's Rufus, and Copilot - don't 'know' your catalog; they retrieve it. Most combine a language model with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG): the system searches indexed web content, shopping feeds, and, for Rufus and Google, live merchant data, then generates an answer grounded in what it finds. What gets retrieved depends on structured, machine-readable facts: schema.org Product, Offer, AggregateRating, and Review markup; accurate feeds in Google Merchant Center and Bing Shopping; and pages that state specs, prices, and comparisons in plain, extractable sentences rather than burying them in images or JavaScript-rendered carousels.

On-site assistants work differently: a chat or conversational-search widget on your own store is typically RAG over your own product catalog, policies, and reviews, so it can answer 'does this run small?' or 'is this compatible with X?' without a human. Quality depends entirely on what you feed it - thin or outdated product data produces a confident-sounding assistant that gives wrong answers, which damages trust faster than having no assistant at all.

The optimization discipline for the off-site side goes by a few names - generative engine optimization (GEO), AI Overview optimization, answer engine optimization (AEO) - and it overlaps with SEO without being identical. SEO optimizes for ranking a page; GEO optimizes for being the fact an AI model cites or paraphrases, which rewards clear, structured, verifiable claims over keyword density or backlink volume. A brand can rank well in Google and still be invisible in AI Overviews, and vice versa, because the two systems weight signals differently. Tools that track how often and how accurately your brand is mentioned across these assistants - Buffy is one - are becoming as necessary as rank trackers were for classic SEO, because you can't fix a visibility gap you can't see.

Step By Step

  1. Audit your structured data. Check product pages for schema.org Product markup with price, availability, SKU, and AggregateRating. Run a sample through Google's Rich Results Test and fix anything failing or partial.
  1. Get your feed into the platforms these assistants actually query. Submit and keep current a Google Merchant Center feed and Bing Shopping feed - both feed Google's AI Overviews/AI Mode product answers. If you sell on Amazon, keep listings complete, since Rufus draws on Amazon's own catalog and reviews.
  1. Write pages that state facts directly. Add or rewrite comparison pages, spec tables, and FAQ sections in plain sentences ('the XYZ jacket is machine-washable and weighs 340g') rather than only in images or infographics - language models extract text, not pixels.
  1. Test your own visibility. Ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Copilot the category-level questions a real shopper would ask ('best waterproof running shoes under $150') and record whether and how your brand appears. Repeat monthly as a baseline.
  1. Close the trust gaps assistants penalize. Make return policy, shipping cost, and availability read identically on your site, your feed, and any marketplace listing - assistants cross-check sources and drop ones that contradict themselves.
  1. Deploy or upgrade an on-site assistant. Point a conversational search or chat tool at your live product catalog, current inventory, and policy documents, not a static FAQ from last year, and scope it to decline answering outside that data rather than guess.
  1. Instrument review and UGC capture. Reviews feed AggregateRating schema and are heavily weighted for both off-site citation and on-site recommendation quality - automate collection at delivery, not just on request.
  1. Track citation rate over time with an AI-visibility monitoring tool, the same way you'd track Google rankings, and treat a drop in mentions as seriously as a ranking drop.
  1. Re-test after every catalog or pricing change. Feeds and structured data go stale fast; a monthly resync keeps assistants from citing outdated prices or discontinued products.

Common Mistakes

Treating GEO as copy-paste SEO. Stuffing keywords into meta tags does nothing for a model doing retrieval - it needs structured facts and clear prose, not keyword density.

Ignoring off-site assistants because 'we don't sell on Amazon or run Shopping ads.' Rufus and AI Overviews pull from broader catalog and web data; skipping feeds doesn't opt you out of being asked about, it just means a competitor gets cited instead of you.

Feeding assistants stale inventory or pricing. An assistant that recommends an out-of-stock item or the wrong price breaks trust immediately, and shoppers rarely give a second chance after being misled.

Shipping an on-site chat assistant with no data boundary. Without a defined scope, it improvises answers about policies or compatibility it has no data for - inventing a discount code or a warranty term turns a helpful feature into a liability.

Optimizing for a single assistant. Teams fixate on ChatGPT and neglect Perplexity, Gemini, Rufus, and region-specific usage; each retrieves differently, and audiences split by geography - Perplexity and Google AI Mode adoption patterns, for instance, differ meaningfully between US and Indian shoppers.

Never testing what assistants actually say. Brands assume SEO gains carry over automatically, then discover months later they're never mentioned, or worse, mismentioned - wrong price, a discontinued variant, or a comparison that favors a competitor.

No clear owner. AI visibility falls between SEO, growth, and CX, so nobody monitors it until a customer says 'ChatGPT told me you don't ship internationally' and it's wrong.

Faq

What's the difference between an AI shopping assistant and a regular chatbot?

A regular chatbot follows scripted rules or a decision tree, like 'Track order' leading to a request for an order number. An AI shopping assistant uses a language model with retrieval, so it answers open-ended questions in natural language - 'which of these two jackets is warmer below freezing?' - by pulling live facts from a catalog, feed, or indexed content instead of matching a pre-written script.

How do AI shopping assistants decide which products to recommend or cite?

Off-site assistants like ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, and Perplexity retrieve from indexed web content and shopping feeds, then favor sources with clear structured data (schema.org markup), facts that stay consistent across sources, and strong review signals. On-site assistants recommend from whatever catalog and inventory data you've connected them to, so incomplete or inaccurate product data produces bad recommendations regardless of how good the underlying model is.

Do I need to sell on Amazon or run Google Shopping ads for AI assistants to show my products?

No, but you need to be discoverable through the channels these tools query. Google's AI Overviews and AI Mode draw partly on Merchant Center feeds and partly on indexed web pages, so a well-structured product page can surface even without paid Shopping ads. Amazon's Rufus is scoped to Amazon's own catalog, so it will only surface your products if you have an active Amazon listing.

How do I add an AI shopping assistant to my own store?

Most ecommerce platforms now offer a native or app-based option, such as Shopify's Sidekick and Shop AI tooling, or you can add a third-party conversational search layer that indexes your product catalog, reviews, and policy pages. The work that actually matters is less about the tool and more about the data behind it: clean product attributes, current inventory, and explicit policy text for it to draw from.

How can I tell if AI assistants are already talking about my brand?

Manually, ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Copilot the kinds of category questions a shopper would ask, and note whether and how your brand comes up, including whether details like price, availability, and claims are accurate. To do this continuously instead of spot-checking, use an AI-visibility monitoring tool that tracks citation rate and accuracy across assistants over time, the same way rank trackers monitor Google positions.
