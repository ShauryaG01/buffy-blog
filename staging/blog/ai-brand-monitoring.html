AI Brand Monitoring: Tracking What Assistants Say About You

Why brands monitor ChatGPT and Gemini daily, what to watch for, and how to respond when AI gets you wrong.

BBuffy Intel2026-07-09 · 7 min read

Overview

Shoppers now ask ChatGPT "what's the best sustainable sneaker brand" or Gemini "is [Brand] legit" before they ever land on your homepage. What these assistants say back shapes consideration the way a Google snippet used to - except you can't see the query, can't see a ranking, and can't buy an ad against it. AI brand monitoring is the practice of systematically asking the assistants your customers actually use the questions they'd actually ask, then tracking what comes back: whether you're mentioned, whether the facts are right, whether the tone favors you, and who shows up next to you in the answer.

This matters right now because assistant answers are volatile - they shift with model updates, new web sources, and even the phrasing of a question - and because almost no brand has real visibility into them yet. You could be quoted the wrong price, attributed a discontinued product, or dropped from a "best of" list entirely, and find out only when a customer asks why. Unlike your website, you can't edit the answer directly; you influence the sources the model draws from, then verify the fix actually landed days or weeks later. Treat this as a new monitoring surface you're standing up from scratch - not a rebrand of the rank tracking you already run for Google.

What To Know

Before you start tracking, get the landscape straight.

Assistants answer three different ways, and each has a different fix. Generative recall pulls from frozen training data - slow to change, you're stuck until the next model update. Real-time browsing/RAG (ChatGPT with browsing on, Perplexity, Gemini) fetches live pages and cites them - this is where fresh content and clean structured data move the needle fastest. Commerce integrations (ChatGPT Shopping, Gemini's shopping graph) pull structured product feeds directly, so getting those feeds accurate updates this channel almost immediately.

Each assistant sources from a different pool. ChatGPT's browsing leans heavily on Bing's index and a set of publishers OpenAI has deals with. Gemini draws from Google's web index plus Google Business Profile and Merchant Center data. Perplexity shows its citations openly, so you can audit exactly which pages fed an answer - use it as a diagnostic tool even if it's not your biggest traffic source. Claude and Copilot weight differently again and tend to hedge more when they lack a confident source.

Two failure modes to watch for: hallucination, where the model states something false with total confidence (wrong founding year, wrong ingredient, a return policy you never had), and omission, where you're simply left out of a comparison you should appear in. Omission is more common and easier to miss.

Geography and language shift answers too. The same question asked from a US session versus an India-based session can surface different retailer citations - Amazon.in or Flipkart listings and local press instead of US outlets - because the browsing layer grounds on region-specific results. If you sell into multiple markets, monitor each one separately rather than assuming one answer represents you everywhere.

Step By Step

  1. Build a prompt set that mirrors real buying questions: branded ("is [Brand] legit"), category ("best [category] for [use case]"), and comparison ("[Brand] vs [Competitor]"). Use 20-40 prompts, not just your brand name.
  1. Run every prompt across the assistants your customers use - at minimum ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity. Test in a logged-out/default session, since that's what most shoppers see.
  1. Log each answer verbatim along with the date, model version, and any cited sources, so you have a record to compare after model updates.
  1. Score every answer on accuracy, presence (mentioned or omitted), and sentiment. Note who else appears and in what order.
  1. Trace wrong answers back to their source - a stale press page, an outdated retailer listing, a Reddit thread - rather than treating the assistant itself as the problem.
  1. Fix the source: update your site copy and schema, correct third-party listings, and publish clear, factual FAQ and comparison content for browsing-based assistants to cite. Buffy can run this test-and-diff process on a schedule and surface which pages across the web are feeding a wrong answer, or power on-site AI answers so shoppers get accurate information straight from you instead of a third party.
  1. Re-run the same prompts after making changes - fixes reach browsing-based answers within days to weeks, and won't touch frozen training data until the next model release.
  1. Set a standing cadence (weekly for priority prompts, monthly for the full set) with a named owner, so this stays a habit rather than a one-time audit.

Common Mistakes

Testing only your brand name. Most damage happens in comparison and "best of" prompts where you're omitted or ranked behind competitors - check those, not just "what is [Brand]."

Checking once and calling it done. Answers drift with every model update; a one-time audit tells you nothing about next month.

Trying to prompt-inject or keyword-stuff your way into a better answer. Assistants weight independent, corroborating sources - third-party reviews, press, structured data - far more heavily than on-page claims about yourself.

Fixing the symptom instead of the source. Editing your own website doesn't help if the assistant is citing a stale retailer listing or an old news article; find and fix the actual cited source.

Ignoring omission because it feels less urgent than a wrong answer. Being left out of a "best [category]" answer costs you consideration just as much as being misquoted, and it's the more common failure.

Treating every assistant the same. ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity source differently and update on different timelines - a fix that moves one won't automatically move the others.

Not routing findings to the team that can actually respond. If an assistant states something false about pricing, safety, or policy, that's a PR, legal, or support issue, not just a marketing dashboard metric.

Skipping regional checks. If you sell in multiple countries, assuming your US answer represents your India or UK answer will miss real, market-specific gaps in what assistants are citing.

Faq

How often should I check what AI assistants say about my brand?

Run your core prompt set at least weekly for high-priority questions (brand reputation, top comparisons) and your full set monthly. Increase frequency around major model releases from OpenAI or Google, and around your own PR moments, launches, or review spikes, since those are when answers are most likely to shift.

How is AI brand monitoring different from social listening or SEO rank tracking?

Rank tracking measures where you sit in a list of links; social listening measures what people say about you. AI brand monitoring measures what a generative model says about you when asked directly - a single synthesized answer with no ranking and often no link back to your site. The inputs overlap with SEO (your web presence, reviews, structured data), but the output and how you audit it are different.

Why does ChatGPT say something wrong about my brand?

Usually one of two reasons: its training data is out of date, so it learned a fact that was true months or years ago and hasn't been updated, or, if browsing is on, it's citing a specific page that's wrong - a stale press release, an old retailer listing, or a forum comment. Check Perplexity, which shows citations openly, to see what kind of source is likely feeding the same error elsewhere.

Can you actually correct what an AI assistant says about your brand?

Not directly - there's no editable listing. You correct it indirectly by fixing or adding to the sources the model draws from: updating your own site and schema, correcting third-party listings and outdated articles, and publishing clear factual content that browsing-enabled assistants can cite. Changes reach real-time/browsing assistants within days to weeks; frozen training data only updates at the next model release.

Which AI assistants actually matter for brand monitoring right now?

ChatGPT and Gemini have the largest reach and should be the priority for most consumer brands, with Perplexity a close third because its visible citations make it the best diagnostic tool. Add Copilot or Claude if your audience skews toward professional buyers researching a considered purchase - those users are more likely to be running research through a work assistant than a consumer chat app.
