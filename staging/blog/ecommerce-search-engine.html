Choosing an Ecommerce Search Engine: Build, Buy, or Native?

The technology decision behind site search - relevance engines, AI search, and what actually matters at each store size.

BBuffy Intel2026-07-09 · 7 min read

Overview

Site search isn't a UX nicety - it's a distinct revenue channel with its own conversion rate, and treating the underlying engine as a solved problem is one of the more expensive mistakes a growing DTC brand can make. According to Baymard Institute's long-running site search usability research, shoppers who use search convert at noticeably higher rates than pure browsers, but only when the results returned are actually relevant to what they typed - irrelevant or empty results cost you the sale on the spot.

The decision you're actually making isn't "do we need search," it's which of three architectures powers it: the native search bundled into your ecommerce platform, a bought relevance engine (Algolia, Klevu, Searchspring, Constructor, Bloomreach, etc.), or a custom-built system on something like Elasticsearch or a vector database. Each has gotten more capable over the past two years as AI/LLM-based semantic and conversational search has moved from novelty to table stakes - shoppers now type natural-language queries ("something for a beach wedding under $100") and expect a relevant answer, not just a list of keyword matches.

This matters right now because the gap between "good enough native search" and "you need a real relevance engine" isn't about brand size or funding - it's about catalog complexity and query intent. Get the tier wrong in either direction and you're either overpaying for enterprise tooling a 200-SKU store doesn't need, or leaking revenue through zero-result searches on a 15,000-SKU catalog that outgrew its platform default months ago. This page walks through how to make that call correctly, and how to revisit it as you scale.

What To Know

Three architectures, three tradeoffs.

Native search ships with your platform - Shopify's Search & Discovery app, BigCommerce's built-in search, and similar. It's free, requires no engineering, and has genuinely improved: Shopify's version now does synonym handling and some ML-based ranking out of the box. Its ceiling is low on merchandising control - you can't easily boost margin SKUs, build complex faceted filters, or run search-specific A/B tests.

Bought relevance engines (Algolia, Klevu, Searchspring, Constructor, Bloomreach, Nosto) are SaaS products purpose-built for commerce search. You get faceting, synonym and typo management, merchandising rules (boost/bury, pin results, out-of-stock handling), and increasingly semantic/AI ranking layered on top of keyword matching. Pricing scales with catalog size and search volume - this is where most brands in the 1,000–50,000 SKU range end up once native search's zero-result rate starts hurting.

Built systems - a custom stack on Elasticsearch, OpenSearch, or a vector database (Pinecone, Weaviate) - make sense when search is inseparable from proprietary data: real-time inventory across dozens of locations, personalization tied to a first-party ML pipeline, or search behavior feeding a recommendation engine you already own. This is an ongoing infrastructure and ML commitment, not a project - budget for a dedicated engineer, not a sprint.

The newer variable is AI search itself: vector/semantic matching that understands intent and synonyms without manual rules, and conversational interfaces that let shoppers ask questions instead of typing keywords. Some brands add this as a layer on top of existing search infrastructure - tools like Buffy sit on your storefront as an AI search and shopping assistant without requiring you to rip out the underlying relevance engine - rather than as a full replacement. A professional evaluation scores each option against your actual catalog size, query mix, and merchandising needs, not against what a competitor twice your size uses. A lean team can run a bought engine well - whether that's two people in Austin or an outsourced engineering group in India - but almost none can maintain a built one part-time.

Step By Step

  1. Pull your current search analytics. Get the zero-result rate (searches returning nothing), the search-to-conversion rate versus site-wide conversion, and your top 50 queries by volume. If your platform doesn't expose this, that's already a signal you're under-tooled.
  1. Segment your catalog by complexity, not just size: SKU count, number of attributes/variants, number of locales or currencies, and turnover rate. A 3,000-SKU catalog with heavy variants (size/color/material) behaves like a much bigger catalog for search purposes.
  1. Classify your query mix. Pull a sample of real searches and bucket them into exact-match (SKU, model number, exact product name), attribute-based ("waterproof black boots size 9"), and discovery/natural-language ("gift for a runner"). Native search handles the first bucket fine; the other two are where bought or AI-layered engines earn their cost.
  1. Match your profile to a tier. Under roughly 500 SKUs with mostly exact-match queries: native is usually fine. Hundreds to tens of thousands of SKUs with faceted or discovery-heavy queries: pilot a bought relevance engine. Tens of thousands of SKUs, multi-locale, or search feeding a proprietary ML/personalization pipeline: build or heavily customize.
  1. Pilot before you commit. Algolia, Klevu, and Searchspring all offer trials or low-commitment tiers - run one against your real catalog and A/B test it against your current search on zero-result rate and search-to-cart rate before signing an annual contract.
  1. If natural-language queries are a meaningful share of your traffic, add an AI search layer (a conversational assistant like Buffy, or your relevance engine's own semantic mode) rather than replacing your whole stack at once. It's lower-risk and easier to measure in isolation.
  1. Set a review trigger, not a review date. Re-run this audit when SKU count crosses a threshold (500, 5,000, 20,000) or when zero-result rate climbs above roughly 5-10% of searches, whichever comes first.

Common Mistakes

Ignoring the zero-result rate. This is the single clearest signal that search is costing you revenue - every zero-result query is a shopper who told you exactly what they wanted and left empty-handed. Check it monthly at minimum; most brands never check it at all.

Buying enterprise search before you need it. A 300-SKU store adopting a full enterprise relevance engine is paying for merchandising rules and facet management it has no catalog complexity to justify. Match spend to catalog complexity and query mix, not to what a larger competitor uses.

Migrating search without migrating the rules. When brands switch engines, they often move products but forget to rebuild synonym lists, boost/bury rules, and redirect logic for common misspellings - search quality regresses right after an "upgrade," and the team doesn't know why.

Testing only on desktop. Mobile search behavior (shorter queries, more typos, heavier reliance on autocomplete) differs from desktop, and a redesign or engine swap tested only on desktop routinely tanks mobile search conversion without anyone noticing until the next monthly report.

Treating "AI search" as one product. A generative chat widget that answers questions is a different thing from a semantic ranking layer that improves relevance for typed queries - brands buy one expecting it to fix the other and end up disappointed with both.

Letting merchandising rules go stale. Boost/bury rules and synonym lists set up at launch don't get revisited as the catalog and promo calendar change, so search quietly starts surfacing out-of-season or out-of-stock items as top results.

Faq

Is Shopify's native search good enough for a small store?

For most stores under roughly 500 SKUs with mostly exact-match or simple attribute queries, yes - Shopify's Search & Discovery app now includes synonym handling and basic ML ranking at no extra cost. Upgrade once you need merchandising rules (boosting margin products, burying out-of-stock items), complex faceting, or your zero-result rate climbs above single digits.

When should a DTC brand pay for a dedicated search engine like Algolia or Klevu?

The trigger is usually catalog complexity and query mix, not revenue size: once you're managing a few thousand SKUs with meaningful variant or attribute depth, running frequent promotions that need merchandising rules, or seeing a rising zero-result rate on discovery-style queries, a bought relevance engine typically pays for itself within a few months of the trial.

What's the difference between keyword search and AI/semantic search?

Keyword search matches the literal words in a query against product text and relies on manually maintained synonym lists to bridge gaps. Semantic (AI) search uses vector embeddings to match intent and meaning, so a query like 'cozy fall sweater' can surface relevant products even if none of them contain those exact words. Most modern relevance engines now blend both - keyword for precision, semantic for recall.

Should I build my own ecommerce search engine in-house?

Only if your search requirements are tied to proprietary data or systems you already own - real-time multi-location inventory, a custom personalization or ML pipeline, or search feeding a recommendation engine - and you can dedicate ongoing engineering time to it, not just a launch sprint. For the large majority of DTC brands, a bought engine delivers comparable relevance quality for a fraction of the maintenance burden.

How do I know if my site search is losing sales?

Check three numbers: zero-result rate (searches returning nothing - anything meaningfully above 5-10% is a red flag), search-to-conversion rate compared to your overall site conversion rate (search users should convert higher, not lower), and click position on your top queries (if shoppers are clicking result 8 instead of result 1, your ranking is off). Any search analytics tool or your platform's built-in reporting should surface all three.
