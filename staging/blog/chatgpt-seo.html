How to Get Your Brand Recommended by ChatGPT

A practical guide to earning ChatGPT recommendations: sources it trusts, content it cites, and how to measure mentions.

BBuffy Intel2026-07-09 · 7 min read

Overview

ChatGPT has moved from novelty chatbot to a genuine discovery channel. When someone asks "what's the best skincare brand for sensitive skin" or "which project management tool should I use," the answer they get shapes a purchase decision before they ever open Google or visit your site. OpenAI has said its weekly active user base is now in the hundreds of millions, and a meaningful share of that usage touches commerce-adjacent questions - product comparisons, "best of" lists, and troubleshooting that ends in a recommendation.

For a DTC brand, this matters because ChatGPT doesn't rank pages the way Google does. It synthesizes an answer from what it learned during training plus, in browsing/search mode, live pages it retrieves and cites. If your brand isn't part of that mix - if you're not mentioned in the sources ChatGPT trusts - you're invisible in a growing slice of purchase research, no matter how well you rank on page one of Google.

The upside: this channel is still early and underoptimized. Most competitors haven't audited their ChatGPT visibility, let alone built a repeatable process for improving it. Brands that treat AI recommendation as a measurable, ongoing discipline - not a one-time trick - will compound an advantage while it's still cheap to earn.

What To Know

ChatGPT answers in two modes, and they behave differently. In pure training-data mode, it draws on what it absorbed up to its knowledge cutoff - a snapshot of the internet's discussion of your brand, frozen in time. In browsing/search mode, which most users get by default now, it retrieves live web pages, reads them, and cites specific sources in its answer. Getting recommended means winning both: being described favorably in text the model was trained on, and being retrievable and citable right now.

Three concepts drive this. Entity clarity - the model needs to know unambiguously who you are: your category, positioning, and differentiators. Inconsistent naming, vague "About" pages, or thin category signals make it harder for ChatGPT to place you confidently in an answer.

Third-party validation - ChatGPT weights independent discussion (Reddit threads, review sites like G2 or Trustpilot, comparison articles, press) more heavily than brand-authored copy, because that's the kind of source it was trained to trust and cite. Your own site matters, but it's rarely the deciding factor.

Structured, direct-answer content - pages that state a clear answer up front ("The best X for Y is...") with supporting detail, FAQs, and comparison tables get cited more often than long narrative copy, because they're easier for the model to extract and quote.

One more thing worth knowing: this isn't identical to SEO, but it's not disconnected from it either. The same authoritative, well-linked, frequently-cited content that ranks in Google is also the content most likely to end up in ChatGPT's training data or get pulled during a live search. Think of it as SEO's cousin - overlapping skills, different scoring system.

Step By Step

  1. Audit your current visibility. Ask ChatGPT the exact questions your buyers would use - "best [category] for [use case]," "[your brand] vs [competitor]," "is [your brand] worth it" - across 15-20 real prompts. Log whether you're mentioned, how you're described, and who shows up instead.
  1. Find out what it's citing. In browsing mode, ChatGPT surfaces source links - open them. If a Reddit thread, a G2 category page, or a competitor's comparison post keeps appearing, that source is actively shaping the answer. That becomes your outreach and content target list.
  1. Get discussed where the model already looks. Claim and fill out listings on the review sites you found (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, or whichever fits your category), and answer relevant questions on Reddit and Quora with genuine, useful responses - not disguised ads.
  1. Publish direct-answer content on your own site. Write comparison pages and "best for X" guides that lead with a clear, quotable answer in the first two sentences, followed by specifics: pricing, ingredients, use cases, who it's not for. Add FAQ sections - they map closely to how people phrase ChatGPT prompts.
  1. Tighten entity signals. Keep your brand name, category, founding details, and key facts consistent across your site, socials, LinkedIn, and press mentions. If you're eligible, a Wikipedia entry is one of the highest-trust sources a model can draw from.
  1. Localize for where the queries actually come from. India is consistently reported as one of ChatGPT's largest markets by user count, and a large share of that usage is professionals researching tools and services for work, not casual browsing. If your content only speaks to U.S. consumer intent, you're missing that demand.
  1. Track mentions on a fixed cadence. Re-run your prompt list monthly, since model updates change answers over time. Tools like Buffy can automate this tracking and flag when your share of AI mentions shifts against competitors, so you're not relying on manual spot-checks.

Common Mistakes

Optimizing only for Google, not for the model. Teams pour resources into classic SEO and assume ChatGPT visibility follows automatically. It often doesn't - a page can rank #1 on Google and never get cited by ChatGPT if it reads like marketing copy instead of a direct, structured answer.

Buying or faking reviews. It's tempting to seed fake five-star reviews on G2 or Trustpilot to speed things up. Platforms and models are both increasingly good at detecting inorganic review patterns, and getting caught costs you trust that's hard to rebuild - with users and with future training data.

Treating it as a one-time project. Running a single audit, publishing a few comparison pages, then moving on. ChatGPT's answers shift as the model updates and as new sources get published; visibility needs the same ongoing attention you'd give paid search bidding or SEO rank tracking.

Ignoring Reddit and forums. Brands focus on their own site and official review platforms while overlooking the informal threads where ChatGPT often finds its most-cited, most-trusted commentary. One well-answered Reddit thread can outweigh a dozen polished landing pages in the model's eyes.

Stuffing your brand name into content unnaturally. Repeating your brand name and target keywords excessively to "train" the model reads as spam to both readers and quality filters, and doesn't reliably improve citation odds.

Measuring once and calling it done. Without a repeatable prompt set tracked over time, you can't tell if a content push actually moved the needle or if you got lucky on one query. Consistent measurement is what turns this into a real channel instead of a guess.

Faq

Does ChatGPT show sponsored or paid brand recommendations?

Not currently in the way search ads work. ChatGPT's recommendations come from its training data and, in browsing mode, from live web sources it retrieves and cites - there's no ad auction deciding which brand gets named. OpenAI has discussed future commerce and shopping features, but today the way to get recommended is to earn a citation-worthy presence across the web, not to buy placement.

How can I check if ChatGPT recommends my brand?

Build a list of 15-20 real buyer questions in your category, including direct "X vs Y" and "best X for Y" phrasings, and run them in ChatGPT on a fixed schedule, ideally in browsing/search mode so you can see cited sources. Track whether you appear, how you're described, and who appears instead. This works manually, but it's easy to let slip - tools built for AI-visibility tracking, including Buffy, can automate the prompt runs and flag changes.

Is getting recommended by ChatGPT the same as ranking well in Google?

No, though the two overlap. Google ranking depends heavily on backlinks and on-page SEO; ChatGPT recommendations depend on whether the model was trained on favorable, authoritative mentions of you and whether it can retrieve and cite a clear, direct answer from your site or third-party sources. A page can rank well on Google and still never get cited by ChatGPT, and vice versa.

Can I pay OpenAI or run ads to get ChatGPT to recommend my brand?

There's no current ad product that buys you an organic-style recommendation inside a ChatGPT answer. The way to influence it is indirect: earn mentions on the review sites, forums, and comparison content the model already trusts and cites, and publish clear, direct-answer content on your own domain.

Why does ChatGPT recommend my competitor instead of me, even though I rank higher on Google?

Usually because your competitor has more third-party discussion - reviews, Reddit threads, comparison articles - that the model was trained on or can retrieve, and their content states a clearer direct answer that's easier to cite. Audit what ChatGPT is actually citing when it recommends them (visible in browsing mode), and target those same sources and content formats yourself.
